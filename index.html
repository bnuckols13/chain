What is Chain Analysis?

Chain analysis is the primary assessment tool in DBT—a detailed, moment-by-moment examination of how problem behaviors unfold. Think of it like a detective investigation: we're looking at all the links in the chain that led from vulnerability to behavior to consequences.

Why Chain Analysis Matters

  • Identifies Patterns: Reveals the specific factors that make behaviors more likely
  • Finds Intervention Points: Shows where we can interrupt the chain
  • Tailors Skills: Helps us know which DBT skills to use when
  • Reduces Shame: Externalizes the behavior to see it objectively

The Seven Components

1Problem Behavior

Described in excruciating detail—specific enough that an actor could recreate it. What exactly happened? When? For how long?

2Vulnerability Factors

What lowered your threshold? Physical states (sleep, hunger, illness), emotional states, stressors, past events that made you more susceptible.

3Prompting Event

The specific trigger that started the chain—often seemingly small. Can be internal (thought, emotion) or external (event).

4Link-by-Link

Each link categorized as: Actions, Body sensations, Cognitions, Events, Feelings. This is where detail matters most.

5Consequences

What happened immediately after? What happened long-term? Understanding reinforcement is key to changing patterns.

6Solution Analysis

For each problematic link: What skill could I have used? What would that have looked like specifically?

7Prevention Plan

How can I reduce vulnerability factors? What repairs are needed? How will I catch it earlier next time?

Key Insights

The "I Don't Know" Phenomenon ▼

Often we genuinely can't remember all the links—that's normal. The practice is staying curious with ourselves, gently asking "what might have been happening?" This itself is a mindfulness practice.

Both/And Thinking ▼

The behavior made sense in that moment AND you want to make a different choice next time. You did the best you could with what you knew AND you're learning new skills. This is dialectical thinking.

Finding the Space ▼

Chain analysis reveals that behaviors aren't inevitable—there are multiple points where intervention is possible. Each link is a potential place to interrupt the pattern. The more we understand our chains, the more choice we have.

Compassion is Required ▼

Without self-compassion, chain analysis becomes self-punishment. The goal isn't to shame ourselves about our patterns—it's to understand them with kindness so we can respond differently. You are doing this work because you care about yourself and your growth.

Common Challenges & How to Meet Them ▼

"I can't remember all the details"
That's normal. Use your best guess. The practice of trying to remember is itself valuable.

"This feels like blame"
Notice if you're judging yourself and bring in self-compassion. The behavior made sense given your state and what you knew in that moment.

"The chain is too long/complicated"
Start with a simpler behavior to practice. You can also do "macro" chains that look at broader patterns.

"I want to skip to solutions"
That's natural—solutions feel better than analysis. But without understanding the full chain, our solutions might miss the real intervention points.
